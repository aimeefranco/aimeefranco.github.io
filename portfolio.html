Mate Roommate Finder Application

Most successful roommmates are ones that are found through friends. Mate is a web application, powered by facebook, that connects people with friends, and friends of friends, who are also looking for roommates. Most websites that help find roommates require that the user fill out a long list of questions that can take upwards of 15 minutes to complete. This is because they are searching through thousands and thousands of potential matches. Mate is able to eliminate this step and use a simple filter because it is only matching you with first, second and third degree connections.

I did lots of drawing and wireframing before building the application. A photo of the potential roommate, and a photo of the apartment are the two main images displayed on the screen. Apartment perks, a way to contact the roommate and added features to filter by neighborhood and by rent are just a couple of things addressed.

The images are of a prototype I created using Bootstrap. I heavily modified the css and used a variety of plugins to get the desired aesthetic and functionality.

Cambodian Water Filter

iDE Cambodia has been distributing water filtration solutions to the Cambodian market for nearly 10 years. Their first product, the Tunsai water filter, has spawned an entire market of competition and vastly increased the availability of clean water to the rural population. After nearly a decade of production, the tooling used for the plastic bucket and lid had reached the end of its life-cycle. iDE wanted to develop a refined product to better serve it's core customer base, provide an attractive option for NGO customers and to perform better in retail sales.

I planned and conducted visits with six rural customers and with five store owners who were either current retailers of the filter or who formerly sold the filter. I learned a lot about what was working well with the current product, and what needed improvement. "Ants lay eggs under the lid so I flipped it over but then it blew away, so I put a box on top of it," was one thing I learned from a rural customer. One retailer we spoke with had lost the contact number for Hydrologic so she couldn't order more even though she was out of stock.

Improved aesthetics, more accessory storage, taller spigot height, tighter gaps and seals, simplified product transport, reduced retail shelf space and better communication with retailers were some of the main points that needed to be addressed in the design.

Four of the concepts were refined, built in CAD and tested with customers again. Though customers liked two of the concepts, there was a clear winner when it came to considering manufacturing costs.

I created paper prototypes and refined the design in SolidWorks. The design addressed all necessary improvements and met a production cost of $6.50.

The filter design has been released for tooling and is expected to hit the market in March of 2015.

Bonfyre Usability Study

I performed a usability study for Bonfyre, a Chicago startup, to better understand active user habits. The study uncovered insights about what is working well for users and where opportunity for improvement lies. The study was separated into three main activities, a usability assessment, a feature use assessment, and a sharing habit assessment.

I recruited users based on their activity within the app. 2-3 users were "super users," or very active, 2-3 were average users or active, and 2-3 were moderate users or moderately active. The sessions were conducted individually and were video recorded.

After 6-8 weeks of using the application, users still found some of the basic functions of the application difficult to use, like starting a new bonfyre (chat) within the application and inviting friends to join it. There were also features that users either didn't use or didn't understand, like the mystery image feature. The name of it scared users and more than one were worried that it would take a random image from their photo album and post it to a group. There were many other findings from the study that will influence future Bonfyre development.

Volunteer Management App

The volunteer management application is a Ruby Gem that is meant to be added to small non-profit's websites to help them manage their volunteers. Many small non-profits are still managing their volunteer events and schedules manually. The application will support these small organizations who are constantly trying to do more with less.

As the UX designer and visual designer on the project, I worked with a front-end developer and a back-end developer to make the application real. Part of testing the application's functionality was making clickable wireframes with InVision App. I learned a lot from watching people use the wireframes and changed the design to make it easier to use.

When I spoke with employees at these organizations, they said that it is important for them to know when volunteers meet certain qualifications. For example, if they're going to deliver food to people in need, they must have their own vehicle and a valid driver's license. When signing up for an event, there is a form to fill out stating that you meet all qualifications for that specific event.

In order to help the non-profits attract as many volunteers as they need, I designed the application to work seamlessly on both desktops and mobile. The application is completely responsive.

CAREBRANCH App for Hospitals

The CAREBRANCH app was developed as a concept to improve communications between hospital staff and patients. Each patient would be able to log into their account and 'meet' their caretakers. They could contact their doctors if they had a questions and could read about their procedure.

Small pieces of information and empowering services can take some of the burden off of patients who are dealing with debilitating medical conditions that can render them feeling powerless, especially while in the hospital.

Cambodian Water Filter for Schools

There was an opportunity to promote and provide safe drinking water for the underserved Cambodian school market. Water that is currently offered to students is considered less ideal (less safe) than water brought from home and is only consumed if alternative beverage options are not available.

The design goal was to develop a high-capacity water filter to compliment Hydrologic's existing line of domestic water filters. To develop a successful high capacity water filter it must hold an appropriate volume of water for a classroom, be able to be maintained by the students, and provide drinking water that is of equal or better quality than their drinking water at home.

I conducted research to better understand the needs for each of the high capacity filter target markets. The research results directly influenced the high capacity water filter design and guided decision making throughout the development process. I visited with schools in Kampong Speu, and Takeo and interviewed directors, teachers and students ranging in age from 4-15 years old.

Students at some schools were drinking untreated pond water even though they knew it wasn't safe to drink. They didn't have any other options. Half of the schools that we visited had filters that were donated within the last 15 years but none of them were still using the filters.

Previous attempts at supplying schools with high capacity filters have not succeeded. A few crucial details will determine the success of a new high capacity filter. Hydrologic staff must be present when recipients receive their filters to educate them on how to use and maintain the filters and to set up a replacement schedule. They also must follow up with recipients to ensure success and make repairs if necessary.

We developed concepts that supported our research findings and that met target production cost. One of the most challenging constraints was to design a high capacity filter that children can maintain. 20 liters was an ideal amount of water, but 20 liters is heavy for an eight year old to carry. Some of our solutions separated the water jugs into two, more manageable, 10 liter jugs.

Hydrologic and iDE are currently pursuing funding for the community filter as a carbon finance project.
